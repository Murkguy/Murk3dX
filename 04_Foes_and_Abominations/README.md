# FOES & ABOMINATIONS

The Murk produces things. The War produced things. Desperation produces things.

What follows is a Director's reference for the threats most likely to fill a session with genuine dread — organized by origin, typed by the [Mob/Operative/Nemesis framework](/10_Directors/#chapter-6-enemy-types), and detailed enough to run without further preparation.

---

## SECTION 1: CREATURES OF THE MURK

The fog did not just kill civilization. It changed what lived in it. Some of what you will find in the Sunken Districts was human, once. Some of it was never meant to exist at all.

---

### THE MOB: MURK-RATS

<div class="flavor-box" markdown="1">

You hear them before you see them. Not one sound — hundreds of small sounds, nails on stone, in perfect synchrony. The fog moves. They come through it like they belong to it.

</div>

**Mob Size:** 8–20. They do not fight alone.
**Pressure:** If the Mob is not reduced below 10 by the end of a round, all players in the zone take 1 Stress (the noise, the movement, the teeth).
**Special:** Murk-Rats carry environmental Murk. Any player bitten (Mob not reduced fast enough) must make a Body Save (DR 10) or gain 1 Stress from toxin exposure.
**Clears at:** 0 remaining.

---

### THE MOB: FOG WALKERS

<div class="flavor-box" markdown="1">

They move wrong. Two legs, two arms, upright — everything correct except the way their heads track. They don't look at the sound. They look at where the sound is going.

</div>

These were human. They are not anymore — the Murk changed them over years of exposure, softening what was rigid and hardening what was not. They do not speak. They do not flee.

**Mob Size:** 4–10.
**Pressure:** Fog Walkers apply no automatic Stress — but they are eerily silent. Players who fail a Perception check before combat begins start the scene Surprised (enemies act first).
**Special:** Fog Walkers are immune to fear-based effects. They cannot be intimidated, routed, or panicked.
**Clears at:** 0 remaining.

---

### THE MOB: CORRUPTED WORKERS

<div class="flavor-box" markdown="1">

Whatever accident fused them to the machines was recent enough that some of them are still trying to use their hands. They reach for you the way a man who has forgotten how to speak reaches for words.

</div>

Factory workers caught in an Esoteric discharge, a Charred One attack, or prolonged Murk saturation. Partially integrated with industrial equipment — press frames, rivet guns, conveyor mechanisms.

**Mob Size:** 4–8.
**Pressure:** Corrupted Workers hit hard. If the Mob is not reduced below 4 before round 2, one random player takes 1d6 Stress from an industrial strike (no save — the weight is simply too much).
**Special:** Heavy Armor is partially effective. Damage dealt by Corrupted Workers ignores Light Armor's threshold. Heavy Armor functions normally.
**Clears at:** 0 remaining.

---

### THE OPERATIVE: THE HOLLOW

A Murkborn who descended too far and did not come back the same way. The Hollow has gone fully native — adapted to the deep fog in ways that make it a predator rather than a survivor.

**Body** d10 / **Mind** d4 / **Spirit** d8
**Predator Die:** d8

**Ability 1 — Fog-Form:** The Hollow can move through dense Murk without penalty. It uses this to close distance in conditions that slow other targets. In low-visibility zones, it acts before players in Initiative regardless of roll results.

**Ability 2 — Silent Strike:** The Hollow's first attack each scene deals +1d6 Stress (the suddenness, not the wound). This bonus is lost if the players have detected it before it acts.

*Goes down at 1 Rank of Suffering.*

**What It Wants:** To remove the intrusion from its territory.
**What It Fears:** Bright sustained light. A Lantern held toward it forces a Spirit Save or it retreats one zone.

---

### THE OPERATIVE: THE LURKER

Something learned to wear a survivor's shape. It speaks. It asks the right questions. It knows how people in distress behave because it has studied them for a very long time.

**Body** d8 / **Mind** d10 / **Spirit** d6
**Mimicry Die:** d10

**Ability 1 — The Face:** The Lurker presents as a survivor or lost Diver. Detecting the deception requires a Perception or Insight check (DR 15). The Esoterist's *The Sight* reveals it automatically — the Lurker reads as Murk.

**Ability 2 — The Question:** The Lurker asks something it shouldn't know. Once per scene, it can reference a personal detail about one player character — something from their background, their name, a relationship. This triggers an immediate Mind Save (DR 12) or 1d4 Stress.

*Goes down at 1 Rank of Suffering.*

**What It Wants:** Unknown. Possibly nothing. Possibly everything.
**What It Fears:** Direct confrontation. Once exposed, the Lurker loses its advantage and will attempt to disengage.

---

### NEMESIS: THE PALE ARCHIVIST

<div class="flavor-box" markdown="1">

The room had been a library. Half the shelves were collapsed. The books were gone — not burned, not rotted, gone, like something had taken them one at a time. In the center of the floor, written in what looked like compressed fog, was a list. Some of the names on it were people you knew. One of them was you.

</div>

Something found in the deepest Pre-Calamity ruins — a library district, an archive complex, an administrative center that catalogued the old empire. It does not attack. It collects. Whether it is an entity, a process, or a fragment of something larger is a question nobody who has encountered it has answered satisfactorily.

**Body** d6 / **Mind** d12 / **Spirit** d10
**Cataloguing Die:** d12
**Starting Potential:** 20

**Passive — The Dread Field:** All players within Near range of the Archivist make a Mind Save (DR 12) at the start of each round or take 1d4 Stress. This is not from anything it does. It is simply from its presence. Stress generated this way cannot be Recovered during combat.

**Active — Documentation:** The Archivist selects one player character and begins recording them. That character's next roll is made publicly — stated out loud before rolling. The Archivist has noted the intention.

**DISASTER TRACK:**

*Rank 1 — It Notices You:*
The Dread Field extends to Far range. The Archivist's documentation becomes targeted — the player it is recording makes all checks at +2 DR this scene. It is paying very close attention.

*Rank 2 — You Are Incomplete:*
The Archivist acts. It is not attacking — it is attempting to finish what it was cataloguing. One player character is targeted with a reach that cannot be blocked or countered: they must make a Spirit Save (DR 15) or lose access to one memory (the Director chooses a piece of their backstory that is now inaccessible to them until the confrontation ends). The character knows something is gone. They cannot say what.

*Rank 3 — The Record Closes:*
The Archivist has what it came for. It begins to withdraw. If the players do not stop it physically, it takes with it everything it documented — which may include things the players did not know it had observed. The Dread Field collapses. The room is suddenly, completely quiet. On the floor where it stood: a list, written in compressed fog. All the players' names. Detailed. Accurate. Including things they never said aloud.

**"If I Win":** The Archivist completes its record and leaves. The players are catalogued. What this means will become clear later.

**Director Note:** The Pale Archivist is not a combat encounter. It can be fought — the stat block supports it — but fighting it is not the point. The point is what it represents: something in the deep Murk that is paying attention, for reasons nobody understands. Use it once, early, to establish that not everything in the dark wants to kill them. Some things simply want to know.

---

### NEMESIS: THE CONGREGATION

<div class="flavor-box" markdown="1">

You found it because you found one piece of it — a small thing, fleshy, that moved with purpose across the floor of the flooded hall and disappeared through a crack in the wall. Then you found another piece, coming from a different direction. Then you understood that the walls themselves were not walls.

</div>

Not one creature. A distributed organism that has colonized several blocks of the Sunken Districts over decades. Individual fragments are Mob-level. The Congregation as a unified entity is something else entirely — and it has been growing.

**Body** d10 / **Mind** d8 / **Spirit** d6
**Coordination Die:** d10
**Starting Potential:** 16

**Passive — Fragments Everywhere:** At the start of every round, the Director places 1d4 Mob-count fragments in the zone (size 1–4 each). These can be cleared normally (DR 5 per fragment) but cannot be permanently reduced — the Congregation produces more. The fragments apply Pressure normally.

**Active — Coordinated Strike:** The Congregation spends 3 Potential. All fragments in the zone act simultaneously on one Initiative count. Every player must make a Body Save (DR 12) or take 1d6 Stress.

**DISASTER TRACK:**

*Rank 1 — It Organizes:*
The fragments stop behaving independently. They move in deliberate patterns, cutting off exits and herding players toward each other. Players who attempt to retreat must make an Athletics check (DR 14) to successfully create distance.

*Rank 2 — One Initiative:*
The Congregation acts as a single entity. All fragments act on the same Initiative count as the Congregation itself, not scattered across the round. It is no longer a swarm. It is one thing with many hands.

*Rank 3 — It Speaks:*
From every fragment, simultaneously, in a voice that is not assembled from sound but from pressure: one sentence. The Director writes this in advance — it should mean something. It knows something about the players or the Sunken Districts that should not be possible to know. After speaking, the Congregation makes its most powerful attack. If it fails (players survive), it withdraws. It has done what it came to do.

**"If I Win":** The players leave. The Congregation remains. The sector belongs to it now.

## SECTION 2: HUMAN THREATS

The most dangerous thing in the Murk is usually another person who also knows what they're doing.

---

### THE MOB: CITY GUARD SQUAD

Novemgrod city guard. Professional, disciplined, carrying standard-issue rifles and sidearms. Not looking for a fight, but trained to finish one.

**Mob Size:** 4–8.
**Pressure:** At 6+ members, the Squad calls for backup at the end of round 2 — a second Squad of 4–6 arrives in round 4. This does not happen if the radio operator (1 member of the Mob, Director's choice) is taken out first.
**Special:** City Guards use cover instinctively. Players must target cover before they can apply Mob reduction — DR +2 if guards are behind established cover.
**Clears at:** 0 remaining, or when they call a retreat (below half strength, no backup incoming).

---

### THE MOB: LOOTER BAND

<div class="flavor-box" markdown="1">

They are not professionals. That is what makes them dangerous. A professional has something to lose. These people decided that what they had to lose was already gone.

</div>

**Mob Size:** 6–14.
**Pressure:** Looter Bands are erratic. At the start of each round, roll 1d6: on a 1–2, one member does something unpredictable (fires blindly into cover, grabs a hostage, runs for a different target). The Director adjudicates.
**Special:** Looter Bands break. When reduced below half strength, make a collective Spirit Save (DR 12). On failure, the remaining members scatter. On success, they become desperate — +1d4 damage on all attacks this round.
**Clears at:** 0 remaining, or when they break.

---

### THE MOB: CHARRED CULTIST CELL

True believers. They are not afraid of death — they consider it a gift, both to receive and to give.

**Mob Size:** 4–8.
**Pressure:** At the end of each round, one Cultist (Director's choice) may voluntarily ignite a carried firebomb, dealing 4 Stress to all players in Close range (no save). This reduces the Mob count by 1 but is not a reduction caused by players.
**Special:** Charred Cultists cannot be routed, intimidated, or persuaded to surrender. The only social check that works on them is one that addresses their theology — and it must be genuine engagement, not deflection.
**Clears at:** 0 remaining. There is no other outcome.

---

### THE OPERATIVE: BRIGAND LIEUTENANT

Commands a Looter Band. Smarter than the people around them, which is not a high bar, but is enough.

**Body** d8 / **Mind** d8 / **Spirit** d6
**Command Die:** d8

**Ability 1 — Hold the Line:** Once per scene, the Lieutenant prevents the Mob from breaking — they automatically pass the Spirit Save that would scatter the band.

**Ability 2 — Target Selection:** The Lieutenant chooses one player character at the start of combat and directs all Mob pressure at that target for the round. That player's Mob reduction DR increases by 2 this round (the Mob is coordinated against them).

*Goes down at 1 Rank of Suffering.*

**Motivation:** Money, territory, or revenge — pick one and make it specific.
**Secret:** The Lieutenant has a way out. A contact in the city, a safe house, an arrangement. They are staying with the band only as long as it is more profitable than leaving.

---

### THE OPERATIVE: RIVAL MERCENARY (SUOMARAN)

Professional. Here for the same job you are. Not necessarily hostile — until the contract says otherwise.

**Body** d10 / **Mind** d6 / **Spirit** d8
**Combat Die:** d8

**Ability 1 — Professional Courtesy:** The Suomaran will not attack first if there is any possibility of a negotiated arrangement. If approached before combat begins, a Parley check (DR 12) can establish terms — split the contract, divide the salvage, define territory.

**Ability 2 — Disciplined Strike:** The Suomaran's attacks are measured and efficient. Their attacks deal full weapon damage with no variance — no dice roll for damage, use the weapon's maximum value. Predictable. Terrifying.

*Goes down at 1 Rank of Suffering.*

**Motivation:** Complete the contract. Collect the payment. Return north.
**Secret:** The contract they're on conflicts with one of the Diver's Code rules. They know this and are trying to find a way to complete it without violating the Code. They will pay well for help.

---

### THE OPERATIVE: OPPOSING COMPANY SOLDIER

A soldier backed by a faction — the Dorian Army, Korovladi private security, Schola field agents. Disciplined, equipped, following orders that the players don't have full visibility on.

**Body** d8 / **Mind** d8 / **Spirit** d6
**Tactics Die:** d8

**Ability 1 — By the Book:** This soldier does not improvise. They follow their orders to the letter, which makes them predictable. Players who succeed on an Insight check (DR 12) can identify what their orders are and anticipate their next action.

**Ability 2 — Call It In:** If the soldier has a working radio and is not neutralized in the first round, they report the players' position. The faction they serve advances their clock by 1 tick.

*Goes down at 1 Rank of Suffering.*

**Motivation:** Follow orders. Go home.
**Secret:** The orders are wrong. They know it. They're following them anyway because they don't see another option.

---

### NEMESIS: THE CONTRACTOR

<div class="flavor-box" markdown="1">

The note was in your safehouse. Not under the door — on the table, next to your gear, written on paper that wasn't yours. It said: *I know where you're going. I'll be there first. — C*

That was three days ago. You've been watching your back since. You found two of his advance markers. You didn't find the third.

</div>

A veteran mercenary captain who has been hired to stop the players specifically. Not to kill them (though that's acceptable) — to stop them from completing whatever they're currently doing. The Contractor has done the research. He knows who they are.

**Body** d10 / **Mind** d10 / **Spirit** d8
**Veteran Die:** d10
**Starting Potential:** 18

**Passive — Prior Intelligence:** Before the confrontation begins, the Contractor has identified one specific vulnerability for each player character — a Weakness attribute, a Burden, a relationship that can be leveraged. He acts on this information.

**Active — Prepared Ground:** The Contractor chooses the terrain whenever possible. In any location where he has arrived before the players, he has set one advantage: a pre-positioned sniper, a rigged exit, a bribed guard, a blocked radio frequency. The Director determines the specific advantage.

**DISASTER TRACK:**

*Rank 1 — Taking Notes:*
The Contractor stops pressing. He retreats to observation range and spends a round watching. He recovers 3 Potential. His next action will be targeted at whichever player has taken the most damage this fight.

*Rank 2 — Personal:*
He has determined which player is the central problem and shifts all focus there. Every attack, every Potential spend, every resource is directed at that player for the rest of the scene. The other players exist only as obstacles.

*Rank 3 — Scorched Earth:*
The contract is not worth dying over — but it is worth burning everything on the way out. The Contractor activates whatever contingency he prepared before the confrontation: the building burns, the evidence disappears, the ally the players were trying to protect is somewhere he can reach before they can. He may not win. He will make sure winning feels terrible.

**"If I Win":** The players' current objective fails. The Contractor is paid. He reports back to whoever hired him. He does not hold grudges — it was professional. He will say hello if they meet again.

**Director Note:** The Contractor is most effective when the players don't know they're being hunted until it is almost too late. Plant the note. Let them find one advance marker. Let them feel the shape of the problem before they see the person behind it. When he arrives, they should already be afraid.

## SECTION 3: THE MOST DANGEROUS THINGS IN THE MURK

A Director's advisory. These are the threats most likely to end a campaign — not because they are the strongest, but because they are the hardest to prepare for.

---

**1. OTHER HUMANS WITH GOOD INTELLIGENCE**

A prepared opponent who knows who you are, what you carry, what your weaknesses are, and where you're going is the most dangerous thing in the game. The Contractor (above) is the cleanest example. But any Nemesis-tier human who has had time to research the players qualifies. Stat blocks do not make enemies scary. Information does.

*Director guidance:* Give at least one human Nemesis per campaign the time and resources to learn about the players before the confrontation. Then use what they learned.

---

**2. THE EIDOLON**

The apex Murk entity — a Daemon that has evolved past the point where conventional categorization applies. Full rules and stat blocks in the [Esoteria chapter](/03_Esoteria/).

*Director guidance:* An Eidolon should appear exactly once per campaign arc, at most. Its presence should feel like a category error — like the game's difficulty has changed modes. The players should not fight it the first time they encounter it. They should survive it, gather information, and return when they understand what they're dealing with.

---

**3. THE CONGREGATION**

Detailed above. What makes it uniquely dangerous: it cannot be cleared. Players who fight it are delaying it, not defeating it. The sector it occupies is gone. The question is always whether the players can get what they need and leave before the Congregation decides they are more interesting than they are worth.

*Director guidance:* Use the Congregation in a location that contains something the players need. Make them weigh the objective against the cost of lingering.

---

**4. THE PALE ARCHIVIST**

Also detailed above. What makes it uniquely dangerous: it wins by observation, not combat. Players who defeat it mechanically have still lost something — it has been documenting them since before they entered the room. Whatever it recorded is now part of its record.

*Director guidance:* The Pale Archivist should appear at least once in the Sunken Districts before it becomes a campaign-level element. Let the players encounter it, survive it, and spend time wondering what it took. Then, later, something uses that information.

---

**5. A NEMESIS WITH A HOSTAGE**

Not a creature type — a situation. A Nemesis who holds someone the players care about has converted all of their mechanical advantages into leverage. Every Potential spend they make is costed against the hostage's safety. Every Disaster Track phase carries the risk of the hostage being harmed.

<div class="rule-box" markdown="1">

**RUNNING THE HOSTAGE SITUATION**

Establish the hostage's status clearly before combat begins: alive, unharmed, in reach of the Nemesis.

At each Disaster Track escalation, the Nemesis's position toward the hostage changes:
- *Rank 1:* The Nemesis acknowledges the hostage explicitly. Negotiation is still possible.
- *Rank 2:* The hostage takes 1 Rank of Suffering. The Nemesis is demonstrating willingness.
- *Rank 3:* The players must choose — finish the Nemesis and risk the hostage, or take the deal the Nemesis has offered.

There must be a deal. A Nemesis with no off-ramp is just a fight. A Nemesis with an offer — even one the players hate — is a scene.

</div>
