# THE DIRECTOR'S HANDBOOK

You are not the storyteller. You are the showrunner.

The difference matters. A storyteller decides what happens. A showrunner builds a world, puts interesting people in it, and then gets out of the way — because the best moments in any show are never the ones the writers fully planned. They're the ones that happen when the characters are put under real pressure and do something surprising.

Your job is to create pressure and be surprised by what your players do with it.

---

## CHAPTER 1: THE SHOW

### THE TELEVISION RULE

The single most useful frame for running *The Murk* is this: **you are making television.**

Not a novel — television. That distinction changes everything.

In a novel, you can afford three pages of atmosphere before something happens. On television, you have about ninety seconds before a viewer picks up their phone. In a novel, you describe a journey across the countryside in rich prose. On television, you cut to the destination and show the dust still settling on the characters' coats.

Apply this ruthlessly:

* **If it wouldn't make the cut, cut it.** Travel montage. Bureaucratic waiting. Uneventful rest. These happen in the fiction — your characters eat, sleep, and walk — but they don't happen on screen. Skip to the part where something is at stake.
* **Every episode should feel different.** A heist episode does not feel like a horror episode. A political intrigue episode does not feel like a firefight. If your players can predict the texture of a session before it starts, you have fallen into a rut. Change the genre. Change the stakes. Change who holds the power. The setting stays consistent; the mood shifts.
* **Vary the cast.** Great television rotates which characters are under pressure. If the same player is always the focus and the others are watching, you're doing one character's show with a supporting cast. Everyone gets a spotlight. Everyone gets a difficult episode.

<div class="rule-box" markdown="1">

**THE EPISODE QUESTION**

Before each session, ask yourself: *What is this episode about?*

Not what happens — what it's *about*. An episode can be about loyalty tested by money. About what you owe the dead. About whether a line exists and where yours is. The plot is the vehicle; the theme is what makes it memorable.

If you can't answer that question, your players will have fun but won't remember the session in three months. If you can, the session has a spine.

</div>

### THE COST OF BOREDOM

Nothing kills a game faster than the table going through motions they don't care about.

The test is simple: **if you wouldn't want to watch it on film, don't show it in play.**

This applies to:
* Combat that has already been decided — if six rounds in, the outcome is not in doubt, call it. Describe the finish. Move on.
* Social scenes where everyone already agrees — if there's no tension in the negotiation, skip the negotiation and jump to its result.
* Exploration where nothing is at stake — searching an empty room is not content. If the room has nothing interesting in it, either put something interesting in it or don't go to that room.

The flip side: **if players are fully invested in a scene, never cut away from it.** The five-round combat limit (more on this shortly) is a tool for dead scenes, not for scenes that are alive. When the table is leaning forward, let it run.

## CHAPTER 2: ENCOUNTERS

### THE SURVIVABLE BUT COSTLY RULE

Every encounter the characters walk into should be **survivable**. None of them should be **free**.

This is the calibration. On one end: encounters that can kill the party outright without warning are frustrating because the players had no agency. On the other end: encounters that pose no real threat are boring because nothing was at stake. The target is the middle — situations where the players have a real chance of success and will definitely pay something to achieve it.

"Costly" does not mean only physical. An encounter can cost:
* **Stress and Suffering** — the characters come out wounded, shaken, or broken.
* **Resources** — ammo burned, Med-Kits used, gear destroyed or lost.
* **Time** — the delay has consequences elsewhere.
* **Relationships** — someone was offended, compromised, or exposed.
* **Information** — the enemy learned something about the players they can use later.

Every encounter should extract at least one of these. If it doesn't, it wasn't a real encounter — it was a speed bump. Either raise the stakes or skip it.

### WHEN NOT TO ROLL

Combat and checks exist to resolve uncertainty. If there is no uncertainty — if the players are clearly capable of handling something and there's no interesting cost — **don't roll for it**.

A group of veteran Divers clearing a single Minor Daemon from a building they've already mapped? That's a sentence of narration, not a combat scene. Save the dice for when failure is genuinely possible and failure matters.

The corollary: **if failure would be interesting, roll for it.** If a character is trying to bluff a guard and getting caught would create a new problem rather than just ending the game, that's a roll worth making. The risk should generate story in both directions.

### DIFFICULTY IS NOT A NUMBER

There is no accurate formula for predicting how difficult an encounter will be. Player creativity, lucky rolls, and unexpected angles will regularly make "hard" encounters trivial and "easy" encounters brutal.

Calibrate by feel, not spreadsheet:
* **One or two enemies per player** is a standard engagement — manageable with good tactics.
* **Outnumbered** means the players need to think before they shoot.
* **Elite enemies** (Armor, high Stress thresholds, special abilities) can anchor an encounter at any numerical ratio.
* **Environmental pressure** (the building is on fire, the fog is advancing, someone is counting down) raises the stakes without adding bodies.

When in doubt: **start scarier than you think you need to, then dial back if it's going badly.** An encounter that starts tough and the players turn around through clever play feels triumphant. An encounter that starts trivial and you artificially add problems to feels rigged.

## CHAPTER 3: CONFLICT

### THE "IF I WIN" RULE

Every conflict — combat, social, political, anything — should have a clear **"if I win, X happens"** statement ready for each side before the conflict begins. Not necessarily spoken aloud, but known to you.

| Side | If I Win... |
| :--- | :--- |
| The Players | They get what they came for, at whatever cost. |
| The Opposition | The players are stopped, removed, or redirected. |

This matters because conflict without stakes is choreography. The moment both sides have something real to lose, every action in the conflict means something. A missed shot isn't just a failed roll — it's the enemy getting closer to what they want.

Before a conflict starts, ask:
1. What does each side want from this specific situation?
2. What happens to the story if each side wins?
3. Is there a middle outcome — a costly victory or a principled retreat — that could be more interesting than a clean win?

**Both sides having a legitimate goal makes the conflict interesting.** The enemy isn't blocking the players because they are obstacles; they're blocking the players because they want something that conflicts. Even a hired guard who just wants to keep his job is a more interesting obstacle than an evil minion who exists to be defeated.

### MOVING THE STORY FORWARD

A conflict that ends without changing anything has failed.

After every significant conflict, something should be different:
* A door that was closed is now open (or closed permanently).
* An ally has been strained or strengthened.
* An enemy knows something about the players they didn't before.
* The players know something about the world they didn't before.
* A resource is gone.

If you can run a conflict, resolve it in the players' favor, and describe the world as functionally identical to before it started — that conflict shouldn't have been in the session.

## CHAPTER 4: PACING

### THE FIVE-ROUND PRINCIPLE

In any scene — combat, social, investigation — five exchanges is enough to determine a winner.

This is not a hard rule. It is a default that prevents the common failure mode: scenes that drag past the point of dramatic tension into mechanical grind.

**How to use it:**
* At the start of a potentially long conflict, decide privately: if this isn't resolved in five rounds, what is the result?
* If five rounds arrive and the scene still has energy — players are engaged, the outcome is genuinely uncertain — keep going. The rule is a floor, not a ceiling.
* If five rounds arrive and the scene has stalled — the outcome is obvious, the players are going through motions — call it. Describe the result. Cut to what happens next.

<div class="rule-box" markdown="1">

**THE CUT**

When you call time on a scene, don't fade to black — cut to consequences.

Not: *"You eventually defeat the guards."*

Instead: *"The last guard goes down. You're standing in the courtyard, breathing hard. One of you has a bad cut across the forearm that's going to need attention. The noise may have carried. What do you do?"*

The cut lands in the immediate aftermath, not after everything has been cleaned up. The mess is the scene.

</div>

### THE MOMENTUM READ

Pacing is a live skill. You are reading the table constantly and adjusting in real time.

**Signs a scene has lost momentum:**
* Players are checking phones or side-talking.
* Combat rounds are taking more than a few minutes per player.
* Nobody is making decisions — they're waiting for someone else to go first.
* The outcome stopped being in question two rounds ago.

**Signs a scene should keep going:**
* Players are arguing tactics — genuinely, because they care about the outcome.
* Someone just made an unexpected move that changed the situation.
* Multiple players are leaning forward.
* The outcome is still genuinely uncertain.

When momentum is lost, **cut**. When it's present, **protect it** — don't interrupt with rules lookups or extended planning sessions that could happen off-screen.

### THE EPISODE ARC

Each session has a natural structure that mirrors television:

**Cold Open:** Start *in* something. Not traveling to the job — in the building. Not receiving the briefing — one sentence of context and then the situation is already happening. The first five minutes of a session should create an immediate problem that demands attention.

**Act One:** Establish what this episode is about. What is at stake, who wants what, where the pressure is coming from.

**Act Two:** Complications. The plan hits reality. Something the players didn't anticipate. A cost they didn't expect. This is where the interesting choices live.

**Act Three:** Resolution — not necessarily victory, but a changed situation. The players know more than they did. The world is different. There is a hook forward.

**The Tag:** End on something that makes the next session feel inevitable. A reveal. A threat arriving. A question that hangs in the air. Leave the players wanting the next episode.

## CHAPTER 5: STORY SEEDS

### USE WHAT THEY GAVE YOU

Every character the players created is a story engine. Their backstory is not background flavor — it is a list of unresolved problems that will become relevant at the worst possible time.

**Backgrounds:** If a player wrote that their Soldier character deserted from a Caranthian mercenary unit, that unit still exists. Someone commands it. Someone remembers the desertion. At some point — when the timing is dramatically interesting — that past arrives.

**Allies:** Every ally on a character sheet is a person with their own agenda, their own problems, and their own capacity to need something from the characters at an inconvenient moment. An ally who only ever gives the players things is a resource, not a character. An ally who needs help, makes mistakes, and occasionally puts the players in impossible positions is someone the players will remember.

**Rivals and Enemies:** The most memorable recurring NPCs are not the ones who want to kill the players. They're the ones who want something that happens to conflict with what the players want — and who are competent enough to be a genuine problem.

### MAKING HOOKS PERSONAL

A job to clear a Diver blockage in Sector 9 is work. A job to clear the same blockage — but one of the players' old contacts was last seen going into Sector 9 — is an episode.

The mechanical content is identical. The investment is completely different.

Before placing any significant encounter, ask: **is there a version of this that connects to someone at the table?** It doesn't have to be direct. A faction that wronged a player's background family, a location tied to someone's origin, a face in the enemy ranks that someone recognizes — these threads are cheap to place and pay off enormously.

You do not need to force resolution. Plant the hook. Let the players choose whether to pull it.

<div class="rule-box" markdown="1">

**THE THREE QUESTIONS (SESSION PREP)**

Before each session, answer these three questions:

1. **What does the world do this session, whether the players are involved or not?** Factions move. Plans advance. Things happen. The world is not waiting for the players.

2. **What is the thing the players most want right now, and how can I complicate their path to it?** Not block — complicate. There is a difference between "the door is locked" and "the door is locked, and the person with the key is someone they have history with."

3. **What happens at the end of this session that makes the next one feel inevitable?** Set the hook before the session ends.

</div>
