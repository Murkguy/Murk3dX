# BOOK I: THE RULES

<div class="flavor-box" markdown="1">
"The fog doesn't just hide things. It changes them. It takes the familiar—a dog, a rifle, a memory—and twists it until it fits the shape of the Murk."
</div>

---

You are going to fail. Understand that first.

Not because you are incompetent. Not because the Director is your enemy. You are going to fail because the world of *The Murk* is designed to grind you down—and grinding is not the punishment. It is the point. Every scar you survive, every bad roll you crawl out of, every moment where you were two bullets short and made it work anyway—that is what this game is.

*The Murk* is not about heroes. It is about professionals. Soldiers, Esoterists, Priests, and Ruffians who are very good at a specific set of things and alive because of that specificity. They descend into the ruins of a dead civilization, drag up what they can, and return to the light a little more broken than when they left. They do it again next week.

These rules exist to make that story feel real. They are designed to reward grit, punish recklessness, and make every success feel earned. Read them carefully. Play them as written the first time. Then, once you understand what they are trying to do, break the ones that don't serve your table.

---

## WHAT IS THIS GAME?

**THE MURK** is a Tabletop Roleplaying Game of industrial horror and attrition. One player takes the role of the **Director** — the architect of the world, the voice of its dangers, and the referee of the rules. Every other player controls a single **Archetype**: a competent, scarred professional descending into the fog-choked ruins of a dead civilization.

It is a game about three things:

1. **Scarcity.** You will never have enough bullets, bandages, or hope. Every resource has a cost, and every cost has consequences.
2. **Attrition.** Damage is sticky. Trauma is permanent. There are no full resets between scenes.
3. **The Spiral.** You are not a superhero. You are a person trying to keep their head above water while wearing lead boots — and the boots are filling up.

If you survive long enough, you become a legend. Most people don't survive long enough.

---

## THE DICE

The Murk uses a specific set of dice that carry meaning beyond their numbers.

* **Two six-sided dice (2d6):** These are always in your hand. They represent the chaos of the world — the variables you cannot control. Wind direction. The guard's patrol schedule. Whether the floor holds.
* **A polyhedral set (d4, d6, d8, d10, d12):** These represent your character's Attributes — raw capacity, trained reflexes, the hard-won skill of a professional. A d4 is a novice. A d12 is someone who has survived long enough to become dangerous at scale.

You will always roll at least three dice at once. This is not an accident.

---

# CHAPTER 1: THE CORE MECHANIC

*Every roll in this game is a negotiation between the world and the person facing it. The world gets two dice. You get one. The world wins more often than not. That is the point.*

---

## 1. THE PHILOSOPHY: FICTION FIRST

In *The Murk*, the story leads the dice — not the other way around. You do not look at your character sheet and ask "what can I roll for?" You describe what your character does, and the Director tells you when the dice matter.

This distinction is everything. Most of the time, your characters succeed without rolling because they are *professionals*. The roll exists to resolve genuine uncertainty — moments where failure is possible, where the outcome matters, and where something real is at stake.

### THE PROFESSIONAL STANDARD

Your characters are not farmers stumbling into adventure. They are Archetypes: competent, dangerous people who have survived things that would break most of the population.

<div class="rule-box" markdown="1">
**When do you NOT roll?**

If the task is routine for someone of your profession, you succeed automatically. No roll required.

- A Soldier field-stripping and cleaning a rifle: success.
- An Esoterist identifying a common magical sigil: success.
- A Ruffian picking a cheap padlock with the right tools and no time pressure: success.

**When DO you roll?**

Only when **all three** of the following are true:
1. **Risk** — failure is possible and meaningful.
2. **Pressure** — something external is making the task harder (time, enemies, conditions).
3. **Stakes** — the outcome changes something.

If failure would just mean "you try again," don't roll. If success is guaranteed for someone of your skill, don't roll. Pick up the dice when it matters.
</div>

### THE TRIGGER: WHEN THE DICE COME OUT

The Director calls for a roll when the situation demands it. Common triggers include:

* An enemy is actively trying to stop you.
* The environment is actively trying to kill you.
* You are attempting something outside your professional comfort zone.
* Time pressure makes a normally routine task genuinely difficult.
* The cost of failure is interesting — not just "you fail," but "you fail *and*..."

The Director should never ask for a roll unless they have a concrete answer to: *what happens if they fail?*

---

## 2. THE ROLL (THE 3dX SYSTEM)

When the outcome is uncertain, you build a pool of three dice.

### THE POOL

<div class="rule-box" markdown="1">

**The Base: 2d6**
Two six-sided dice represent the world's baseline chaos. Statistically, 2d6 clusters around 7 — competent professionals should succeed at reasonable tasks. This is deliberate. The world is hard, not impossible.

**The Attribute Die: 1dx (d4–d12)**
Your training, talent, and specialization. This die is determined by the Attribute being tested:
- **BODY** for physical feats, endurance, and melee.
- **MIND** for intellectual tasks, precision, and deduction.
- **SPIRIT** for force of will, social influence, and resisting corruption.
- **Your Unique Attribute** (Tactics, Arcana, Devotion, Guile) for Archetype-specific actions.

A character with a d4 in an Attribute is a novice. A d12 is world-class.

</div>

### THE FORMULA

<div class="rule-box" markdown="1">
**Result = 2d6 + Attribute Die + Modifiers**

Compare your Result to the **Difficulty Rating (DR)** set by the Director.

| DR | Name | What It Represents |
|:--|:--|:--|
| **10** | Standard | Challenging but expected for a professional. Shooting a moving target, climbing a wet wall under fire, decoding a cipher in dim light. |
| **15** | Hard | A genuine feat of expertise. Picking a guild-vault lock, resisting a magical compulsion, tracking a target through a crowd in the rain. |
| **20** | Impossible | A feat of legend. Surviving a fall from a watchtower, convincing a Murk-beast to hesitate, holding a door against twelve armed men. |

</div>

### MODIFIERS

Modifiers are flat bonuses or penalties added to your Result. They come from Skills, Talents, conditions, and circumstances. Common sources:

* **Trained Skill:** +2 to relevant rolls.
* **Expert Skill:** +4 to relevant rolls.
* **Suffering Penalty:** -1 per Rank of Suffering (cumulative).
* **Environmental Penalty:** -2 for severe conditions (fog, darkness, extreme weather).
* **Bonus Die:** Some abilities add +1d6 to the roll. Roll it and add the result.

The Director should never stack more than two situational modifiers at once. The dice already carry variance. Piling on penalties turns rolls into math problems.

---

## 3. THE OUTCOMES

The dice do not just tell you if you succeed. They tell you *how*, and *at what cost*.

### SUCCESS (Roll ≥ DR)

You achieve your goal. The door opens, the shot connects, the target gives you the answer. The scene moves forward on your terms.

A successful roll does not mean you look good doing it. A Ruffian who just barely hits DR 10 on a lockpick roll: the lock opens, but it takes a tense, sweating minute and the pick is bent. A Soldier who rolls a 16 against a DR 10 combat task: clean and professional. The *how* is yours to describe; the Director narrates the world's response.

---

### FAILURE (Roll < DR)

You do not achieve your goal cleanly. But failure in *The Murk* is never a dead stop.

<div class="rule-box" markdown="1">

**THE FAIL-FORWARD PRINCIPLE**

A failed roll always advances the story. The Director chooses one:

**Narrative Failure** — The task simply doesn't work. The door is solid. The target doesn't believe you. The machine won't start. *But something else happens as a result.* Time passes. An enemy patrols by. A noise is made.

**Success at a Cost** — You achieve the goal, but something goes wrong in the process. The door opens, but the noise echoes through the building. You convince the guard, but he asks for your name. The machine starts, but it vents a cloud of steam that ruins your concealment.

The Director should never respond to a failed roll with "nothing happens." Nothing is always the worst outcome.

</div>

Failure also generates **1 Potential** (see Chapter 2). Falling short of the mark fuels the momentum that lets you transcend it later.

---

### THE CRUSHING SUCCESS (Roll ≥ DR + 10)

You exceed the difficulty by 10 or more. You do not just succeed — you dominate.

A Crushing Success is a moment of competence so complete that the world bends around it. You are not just good; you are *exactly* good enough for this exact moment in a way that will matter.

* **In Combat:** Add an extra Damage Die to the attack.
* **On a Skill Check:** Choose one:
  * You complete the task in half the expected time.
  * You gain a key insight the Director must reveal. *(You pick the lock, and you notice the tumblers were oiled recently — someone came through here in the last hour.)*
  * You leave no trace. No one will know this happened.

Crushing Successes also generate **1d4 Potential** (The Crush). Excellence feeds the engine.

---

### THE BOTCH (Fail + Natural 1 on the Attribute Die)

If your Total is a Failure **AND** your Attribute Die shows a naturally rolled 1, you have Botched.

<div class="flavor-box" markdown="1">
*This is not bad luck. This is the Murk reaching up through your competence and yanking.*
</div>

A Botch is a catastrophe — not merely a failure, but a failure that makes things actively worse. The Director does not invent the disaster; they reveal the one that was always waiting for a moment of distraction.

**Common Botch Results (Director's discretion):**

| Context | Example Botch |
|:--|:--|
| Firearms | The weapon jams or misfires. Requires an Action to clear. |
| Lockpicking | The pick snaps off in the keyhole. Lock is now permanently harder or impossible. |
| Athletics | You slip. You go where you did not intend to go. |
| Social | You say exactly the wrong thing. The NPC's disposition worsens permanently. |
| Magic | The formula misfires. Something nearby suffers the effect instead. |
| Stealth | You are made. The alarm is raised. |

**On a Botch:** You do not gain Potential from the Grinder. There is no silver lining. The Botch *is* the silver lining — the universe reminding you that it is not, and has never been, on your side.

You may spend **5 Potential** to downgrade a Botch to a standard Failure. This is often worth it.

---

## 4. THE EXPLODING DIE

*Optional Rule — Recommended for all play.*

If your Attribute Die rolls its **maximum value** (a 4 on a d4, an 8 on a d8, a 12 on a d12), the die **Explodes**.

<div class="rule-box" markdown="1">
**Exploding Die Mechanic:**
1. Note the maximum result.
2. Roll the same die again.
3. Add the new result to the running total.
4. If it hits maximum again, repeat.

There is no cap.
</div>

### WHY THIS EXISTS

The Attribute Die is the die of competence. A d4 Attribute is a rank amateur. By raw math, a d4 character averages a total around 9 — they will fail Standard tasks roughly half the time. The Exploding Die gives every character a statistically small but real chance to exceed themselves on any given roll.

It is not likely. It is *possible*. That is the difference between an amateur and a corpse.

A novice Esoterist with Arcana d4 rolling to resist a Murk-entity: average result is 9. But on a 4-4-3, their total is 14. They held something back that no one expected. Including them.

**The Attribute Die also applies to Saves** (see Chapter 3: Stress & Suffering). The Exploding Die applies there too. It is the mechanical representation of *refusing to break*.

---

# CHAPTER 2: THE ECONOMY OF SURVIVAL (POTENTIAL)

*In other games, heroism is a starting condition. In The Murk, it is a resource you generate by suffering and spend to survive.*

---

Potential is not inspiration. It is not fate points. It is not luck in the abstract. **Potential is pressure converted to momentum** — the mathematical representation of what happens when a professional refuses to quit.

It accumulates from failure and crisis. It depletes when you need it most. The cycle is not incidental; it is the core loop of *The Murk*. You will walk into a scene with nothing and claw your way to twelve Potential by making bad rolls and watching your flaws torpedo your plans. Then you will spend those twelve Potential in a single catastrophic moment to survive something that should have ended you.

That is the game.

---

## 1. POTENTIAL CAP

Your Maximum Potential is your ceiling — how much pressure you can hold before it either releases or breaks you.

<div class="rule-box" markdown="1">

| Rank | Name | Max Potential |
|:--|:--|:--|
| **1** | Green | 12 |
| **2** | Seasoned | 16 |
| **3** | Veteran | 20 |
| **4** | Legend | 24 |

</div>

### THE COLD START

Every new Episode begins at **0 Potential**. No carryover. No reserves. The session opens with your characters at their most vulnerable — professionals stripped of their momentum, forced to rebuild it under fire.

This is intentional. The early scenes of an Episode should feel precarious. The late scenes should feel like a pressure cooker. Potential is the valve.

### RANK

Rank is your character's place in the world's hierarchy of survival. It does not represent a title or a promotion — it represents how many times you have descended into the Murk and returned. Rank increases through XP milestones and changes more than your Potential cap. Higher Ranks unlock access to Tier 2 and Tier 3 Talents.

---

## 2. GAINING POTENTIAL (THE CYCLE)

You gain Potential in four specific ways. Potential generated beyond your Hard Cap is lost — the pressure dissipates before you can use it.

---

### A. THE GRINDER
*"We learn more from our failures than our victories." — attributed to several dead soldiers*

<div class="rule-box" markdown="1">
**Trigger:** You roll the dice and Fail (Total < DR).
**Gain:** 1 Potential.
**Exception:** You do not gain Potential from a Botch.
</div>

The Grinder is the backbone of the Potential economy. Competent professionals fail constantly in *The Murk* — not because they are bad at their jobs, but because the world is harder than the jobs. Every failed lockpick, every missed shot, every failed persuasion attempt teaches you something. The system encodes this as momentum.

The practical effect: the worse a scene goes, the more Potential you generate. A session where everything goes wrong is a session where your characters become capable of something extraordinary before the end.

---

### B. THE SURGE
*The chaos, for once, aligns in your favor.*

<div class="rule-box" markdown="1">
**Trigger:** Your 2d6 show **Doubles**.
**Gain:** Potential equal to the **face value** shown.
- Double 1s → 1 Potential (and possibly a Botch)
- Double 3s → 3 Potential
- Double 6s → 6 Potential

**Note:** The Surge triggers regardless of whether the roll succeeds or fails.
</div>

The Surge is the wild variable. It is not reliable and it cannot be engineered. Doubles are random — the universe occasionally hands you something you did not earn and did not deserve. On a Double 6, you walk away from that roll with a full sixth of your Rank 1 cap for free. On a Double 1, you walk away with 1 Potential and almost certainly something worse.

The Surge rewards play style that maximizes number of dice rolled — more rolls, more chances at doubles.

---

### C. THE BURDEN
*Your damage is not dead weight. It is ammunition.*

<div class="rule-box" markdown="1">
**Trigger:** Your Burden (Flaw) significantly complicates the scene — it is not just inconvenient, it *matters*. Your bad leg costs you the chase. Your Debt sends collectors at the worst moment. Your addiction makes you miss the window.
**Gain:** Roll **1d6 Potential**.
</div>

The Burden mechanic is the system's answer to the question: *why would I want a flaw?*

Taking a Burden at character creation gives you an extra Talent. Activating a Burden mid-session gives you 1d6 Potential — an average of 3.5, which is 7% of your Rank 1 cap from a single complication. But the Burden only triggers when it genuinely costs you something. The Director decides when a Burden is "significant." A Limp that slows you down slightly is not significant. A Limp that puts you at the back of a chase just before the gate closes — that is significant.

The design logic: *your flaws are the most interesting things about your character. The system pays you to use them.*

---

### D. THE CRUSH

<div class="rule-box" markdown="1">
**Trigger:** You achieve a Crushing Success (Total ≥ DR + 10).
**Gain:** Roll **1d4 Potential**.
</div>

Excellence generates momentum. A Crushing Success is rare enough that the reward is meaningful — an average of 2.5 Potential for a feat that required genuine commitment. It also doubles as the game's positive feedback loop: skilled characters succeed bigger, which generates Potential, which lets them succeed bigger still. The spiral works in both directions.

---

## 3. SPENDING POTENTIAL (THE COST OF HEROISM)

You may spend Potential at any time — even on another player's turn, if it makes narrative sense. Potential is the currency of *refusal*: refusing to fail, refusing to break, refusing to let a bad roll stand.

<div class="rule-box" markdown="1">

| Spend | Cost | Effect |
|:--|:--|:--|
| **BOOST** | 2 Potential | Add **+1** to any roll. Stackable — 10 Potential spent = +5. |
| **ACTIVATE** | 5 Potential | Regain **1 Class Resource** (Command Point, Favor, Agony, or Token). |
| **MITIGATE** | 1 Potential | Reduce incoming **Stress** by 1, before it hits your track. |
| **NEGATE BOTCH** | 5 Potential | Downgrade a Botch to a standard Failure. |

</div>

### THE DECISION

The single most important tactical question in *The Murk* is: **spend now or save for the conversion?**

Unspent Potential at the end of an Episode converts to XP (see below). A character who ends the session with 12 Potential walks away with 12 XP — enough to buy a Tier 1 Talent. A character who spent everything surviving the last fight walks away with 0 XP but is still alive to spend it.

Neither approach is correct. The pressure to choose is the game.

**General guidance:**
* **Boost** is efficient for important rolls but wasteful on trivial ones. Use it when the DR matters.
* **Activate** is emergency fuel — you are out of Command Points mid-combat or out of Favor in a crisis. It hurts to spend 5 for 1 resource, but it is better than having neither.
* **Mitigate** is the most underrated spend. Reducing incoming Stress by 4 costs 4 Potential, which is cheap insurance against a Stress spiral — and spending it in 1-point increments means you never over-commit.
* **Negate Botch** should almost always be taken. A Botch is typically worse than 5 Potential is worth. The exception: late in a session when conversion is more valuable than the Botch's consequence.

---

## 4. THE CONVERSION (SURVIVAL INTO GROWTH)

At the end of an **Episode** — a full session, or a completed mission arc — the adrenaline fades. What remains are the lessons.

<div class="rule-box" markdown="1">
**The XP Rule:** Any Potential remaining in your pool at the end of the Episode converts to **Experience Points (XP)** at a **1:1 ratio**.

**The Reset:** Your Potential drops to **0** at the start of the next Episode.
</div>

### SPENDING XP

XP is spent to unlock Talents from your Archetype's tree and to advance your Skills from Trained to Expert.

| Purchase | Cost |
|:--|:--|
| Tier 1 Archetype Talent | 3 XP |
| Tier 2 Archetype Talent | 6 XP |
| Tier 3 Archetype Talent | 9 XP |
| Skill: Trained → Expert | 4 XP |
| Advance Devotion Die (Acolyte only) | 5 XP |

*XP thresholds for Rank advancement are set by the Director based on campaign scope. A short campaign might Rank up every 2 Episodes; a long one, every 5.*

# CHAPTER 3: STRESS & SUFFERING (OVERVIEW)

*The full rules for Stress, Saves, Suffering, and Recovery are in [Health & Suffering](../11_Conflict and Hazards/HAZARDS.md). This section covers the fundamentals you need before the first roll.*

---

The Murk does not have hit points. It has **Stress**.

Stress is a single number that measures everything that is going wrong with you at once: the bullet graze, the monstrous thing you saw in the fog, the three hours you spent holding your breath in a flooded corridor. It accumulates from all sources simultaneously. There is no "physical health" separate from "mental health." You are one person. You have one threshold.

### HOW IT WORKS

<div class="rule-box" markdown="1">

1. **Take damage** (from any source). Add the amount to your **Stress Track**.
2. **Immediately roll a Save**: 3 Attribute Dice matching the damage type.
   - *Physical damage (bullets, blades, falls):* 3 Body Dice
   - *Mental damage (fear, horror, magical backlash):* 3 Mind Dice
   - *Spiritual damage (corruption, soul attack):* 3 Spirit Dice
3. **Compare your total roll to your current Stress.**
   - **Roll > Stress:** Shrugged off. The Stress remains, making the next hit harder.
   - **Roll ≤ Stress:** Take **1 Rank of Suffering**. Reset Stress to 0.

</div>

### THE SPIRAL

Suffering imposes a **cumulative -1 penalty to all rolls** per Rank. At 1 Rank of Suffering, you are hurt and shaken. At 2, you are operating on nerve endings. At 3 Ranks, you roll to see if you Break.

The Stress system creates a spiral: damage makes you more likely to take more damage, which makes the penalties worse, which makes future Saves harder. The only exits from the spiral are retreat, healing, or Potential spent to Mitigate incoming damage before it hits.

### RECOVERY

Suffering does not reset at the end of the night. You remove **1 Rank of Suffering** at the end of each Episode. A doctor can downgrade a Critical debility to a Hinderance during downtime. Some Miracles and Talents can accelerate recovery.

*See [Health & Suffering](../11_Conflict and Hazards/HAZARDS.md) for the full Suffering tables, the Break State, and recovery rules.*

# CHAPTER 4: THE DISASTER TRACK

<div class="flavor-box" markdown="1">
*"The fog does not attack in a straight line. It circles. It waits. And the moment you stop paying attention to how much of it has gotten inside — that is the moment it wins."*
</div>

---

Not every failure is a failed roll. Some failures are architectural. A plan collapses, a key ally is lost, someone makes a choice that cannot be undone. These moments do not just hurt the characters — they damage the mission. They narrow the possible futures.

The **Disaster Track** is the Director's measure of how much that narrowing has occurred. It is a number from **1 to 10**, tracked openly at the table on a piece of paper, a die, or a dedicated marker. Everyone can see it. That is intentional.

At low numbers, it is a warning. At high numbers, it is a clock. At 10, the clock runs out.

---

## 1. THE TRACK

<div class="rule-box" markdown="1">

| Track | Tier | Mechanical Effect |
|:--|:--|:--|
| **1–3** | Deteriorating | Narrative pressure only. No mechanical penalty yet. |
| **4–5** | Compromised | +1 to all DRs. One backup option or escape route closes. |
| **6–7** | Dire | +2 to all DRs. Max Potential reduced by 2 for all characters. Reinforcements are not coming. |
| **8–9** | Critical | +3 to all DRs. At the end of each Scene, every character takes **1d4 Stress** from accumulated pressure. |
| **10** | The Reckoning | See below. |

</div>

The DR penalty is cumulative within a tier but does not carry over between them. At Track 4, all DRs are 1 higher than normal. At Track 6, they are 2 higher. At Track 8, they are 3 higher. These are not additive — the Track tells you the current penalty, not a stacking total.

---

## 2. ADVANCING THE TRACK

The Director advances the Track. It is not automatic; it requires judgment. The Track represents *meaningful* compounding failure, not every bad roll.

### ADVANCE BY +1 WHEN:

* A player **Botches** a mission-critical roll — one where the outcome directly affects the objective, not a peripheral skill check.
* The players make a **grievous decision** with foreseeable consequences, and those consequences arrive. *(The Director should telegraph the risk before the choice is made. The Track is not a punishment for being surprised — it is a consequence of being warned and proceeding anyway.)*
* A **primary objective** is partially compromised — a witness escapes, a route is burned, a cache is emptied before the players arrive.
* The players **fail a Reckoning Save** at the end of a Critical Scene (Director's discretion for high-stakes setpiece moments).

### ADVANCE BY +2 WHEN:

* Two or more Botches occur **in the same Scene**.
* An **irreversible catastrophe** occurs — the hostage is dead, the artifact is destroyed, the ship goes down with the only map aboard.
* The party **breaks formation** in a way that directly causes a teammate's capture or death.

### THE DIRECTOR'S RESTRAINT

The Disaster Track is a tool, not a punishment engine. Advancing it too freely removes its dramatic weight. A single session should rarely see more than 3–4 advances under normal play. Reserve +2 advances for moments the whole table will remember. If the Track hits 8 by the second scene, the game stops being tense and starts being hopeless — which is a different, worse experience.

---

## 3. REDUCING THE TRACK

The Disaster Track can be reduced. The mission is not simply a slide toward failure — competent professionals who respond to crisis can claw back the margin.

<div class="rule-box" markdown="1">

| Method | Reduction |
|:--|:--|
| Complete a **primary mission objective** | −1 |
| Players collectively spend **5 Potential** at the table (distributed as they choose) | −1 |
| **Acolyte Petition** (Threshold 10, costs 2 Favor) | −1 |
| A significant **downtime** between missions | Resets to 0 |

</div>

**Collective Potential spend** requires the group to agree and is one of the few mechanics that explicitly rewards cooperation over individual optimization. Spending 5 Potential to reduce the Track by 1 is often a poor exchange mathematically — but math is not always the point.

**Objective completion** is the primary intended reduction. Missions should be structured so that completing secondary objectives — rescuing the witness, securing the exit route, finding the secondary cache — naturally reduce the Track. This gives players agency over the pressure they are under.

---

## 4. THE RECKONING (TRACK = 10)

When the Track reaches 10, the Director calls The Reckoning.

The Reckoning is not the end of the session. It is the end of the mission's clean version of success. The players have reached a point where the margin is gone — where the fog has gotten all the way inside, and now the question is how much of themselves they can carry out.

### THE RECKONING ROLL

The Director rolls **1d10** openly.

<div class="rule-box" markdown="1">

| Roll | Outcome |
|:--|:--|
| **1–2** | **Total Failure.** The mission fails. The objective is lost. The characters may survive — beaten, empty-handed, haunted — but what they came for is gone. |
| **3–5** | **Costly Failure.** The group may salvage **one** objective. The Director names what is available. Everything else is forfeit. |
| **6–8** | **Desperate Salvation.** Success is achievable — but the Director names a price before the group decides whether to pay it. *(A character is captured. A key resource is destroyed. Someone has to stay behind.)* |
| **9–10** | **Against All Odds.** They make it. The Director describes exactly how little margin remained — and the table should believe it. |

</div>

### PLAYER INPUT AT THE RECKONING

After the Director rolls but **before** the outcome is resolved, the players may spend Potential one final time:

* Spending **5 Potential** shifts the roll result **+1** (a 2 becomes a 3, a 5 becomes a 6, etc.).
* This can only be done **once**, from a single character's pool.
* It cannot shift the result above 10.

This gives the players a slim but real mechanical lever at the worst possible moment — which is, again, the point.

---

## 5. THE DISASTER TRACK AND THE GRAND RITE

The Esoterist talent **The Grand Rite** (Tier 3) references an internal Disaster Track for catastrophic ritual magic. When casting the Grand Rite, failed Arcana rolls advance **the session's Disaster Track** directly — not a separate internal counter. Each failed check during the Rite advances the Track by +1.

This is by design. The Grand Rite is the most dangerous spell in the game. Its failure is not just personal — it damages the mission. An Esoterist who attempts the Grand Rite under pressure is betting the entire table on their Arcana die.

---

## 6. STARTING THE TRACK

The Disaster Track begins at **0** at the start of every Episode. It has no mechanical effect at 0 — the first advance to 1 signals that the mission's margin has begun to erode.

Directors may choose to begin certain high-stakes Episodes at 1, 2, or 3 to represent a mission already under duress — a rescue that started late, a job where the client withheld information, a dive into a sector the last crew never came back from.

*Starting the Track above 0 should be communicated to players upfront. They should know the hole they are already in.*
