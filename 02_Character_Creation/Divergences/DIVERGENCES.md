# CHAPTER 3: THE DIVERGENT (SPECIES)

In the years since the Calamity, humanity has branched. Some have remained unchanged, clinging to normalcy as if the world hadn't ended. Others have been altered — by the fog, by the factories, by the old blood of the wild, or by choices made generations before they were born.

Your species is not a power tier. It is a lens: the world sees you through it, and you see the world through it. Two characters of different species in the same room are not playing the same game.

---

## 1. HUMAN
**The Desperate Majority.**

Humans are the baseline of Tellus — the refugees of Ruthenia, the factory workers of Carantha, the merchants of whatever used to be the lowland cities. They do not have the instinctive advantages of the altered kin. What they have instead is a terrifying, almost pathological refusal to accept that the world has ended. Where a Murkborn adapts and a Chimera instincts, a Human *decides*. That act of will, repeated under impossible pressure, produces the most dangerous thing in the Murk: someone with nothing left to lose and a plan.

* **THE SPARK (Ambition):** Humans push harder than any other kin. Gain **+1 to your Maximum Resource Cap** (Command Points, Agony, Favor, or Tokens).

**On the Shelf:** Humans are the majority and the default. They run the Merchant Houses, fill the Guard rosters, and occupy most positions of political power in Novemgrod. This is not because they are superior — it is because there are simply more of them, and because the institutions of the old world were built by and for them. A Human character moves through most social situations without friction. They are the baseline that every other species is measured against.

**In the Murk:** Adaptability. Humans survive in the deep Murk by planning, by preparation, by refusing to give up when the gas filters start running low. They do not have the Murkborn's immunity or the Anthroparion's plating. What they have is the ability to decide that dying today is unacceptable and *mean it*.

## 2. MURKBORN
**The Children of the Fog.**

Born in the settlements deep within the Murk's reach — or to parents who were — the Murkborn carry the fog in their blood. They are pale, often hairless, with wide eyes that catch light like a cat's in the dark. To a Murkborn, the toxic fog is not an enemy. It is a neighbor. They feel the absence of it in the clean air of the upper peaks as a kind of pressure, a low hum of wrongness that never entirely goes away.

The oldest Murkborn settlements exist in the mid-fog zone — too deep for most to survive, not deep enough to be truly hostile. They build there, trade there, live there, and die there of the same things humans die of: violence, starvation, old age. Not the fog.

* **FOG-LUNG (Immunity):** You are immune to the environmental toxicity of the Murk. You do not make Body Saves for breathing the fog, and you do not suffer Lung Rot. *(Note: The psychological effects of the Murk — The Whisper, the fog's mental pressure — still apply. The fog recognizes you. It does not necessarily spare you from itself.)*
* **STIGMA (The Outsider):** Civilized society fears and resents you. Increase the Difficulty of all Social checks with "Civilized" NPCs (Nobles, Merchants, Guards) by **+2**. They assume you are contaminated, a spy for the fog, or simply wrong in ways they can't articulate.

**On the Shelf:** The upper peaks of Novemgrod were not built with Murkborn in mind. The architecture assumes you need light. The social structures assume you are afraid of the dark. A Murkborn on the Shelf exists in a state of constant low-grade friction — not violence, usually, but the kind of exhausting wariness that comes from knowing that half the room has already decided what you are before you open your mouth.

**In the Murk:** Home. A Murkborn descending into the fog saves resources that other characters spend on filter cartridges and Body Saves. They can move faster, stay longer, and navigate by senses that humans don't have in the deep zone. The tactical advantage is real. Whether it compensates for the social cost above depends entirely on the campaign.

## 3. ANTHROPARION
**The Souls in Iron.**

No one knows who built the first ones. The answer died in the Black Day, along with most of the records. What remains are the Anthroparions themselves — iron-framed, wood-jointed, ceramic-faced — walking proof that souls have no preference for flesh over metal, wood over bone.

Whether through ancient Vardan soul-binding rituals, radical Carantha experimental surgery, or something older that the histories won't name, these are human souls anchored to chassis of manufactured material. They do not breathe. They do not bleed. They cannot eat, though some maintain the habit of picking up food and setting it down again, a relic behavior from before the transition. They feel fear. They feel grief. They love, argue, and break under psychological pressure just as reliably as anyone made of meat. They simply do not do it with a heartbeat.

* **PLATING (Iron Skin):** Your body is a fortress. You have natural **Armor 1** — reduce all Physical Stress taken by 1, to a minimum of 0.
* **INORGANIC (Scarcity):** Standard medicine fails you. Med-Kits and Potions have no effect. To remove Stress or Suffering, you require a **Repair Kit** and an ally with at least a Trained Skill in Tech or Medicine. *(This makes you dependent on resources that are harder to find than bandages — factor this into group composition.)*

**On the Shelf:** The Anthroparion occupy an unusual legal gray zone. The pre-war laws were written for biological persons. Most have been amended, grudgingly, to cover constructs with verifiable soul-anchors. In practice, this means Anthroparions have legal personhood in Ruthenia and parts of Carantha, limited recognition in Pyrthia, and none whatsoever in the Kemet remnant territories or the Suomi interior. A Novemgrod Anthroparion carries papers that most guards accept, most merchants tolerate, and most priests argue about in private.

**In the Murk:** The deep Murk's toxicity does not affect them. They do not need to breathe. They do not require food, water, or warmth at a biological level — though the chassis degrades under sustained cold. What they need is maintenance. A long expedition without a Tech-trained ally is not a death sentence for an Anthroparion — it is a slow accumulation of grinding damage, loose joints, and degraded motor response, until something critical seizes and they cannot move at all.

## 4. CHIMERA
**The Wildtouched.**

Old pacts. Wild blood. Magical fallout from the War. The cause doesn't matter, and most Chimeras have stopped asking. What matters is that something in them has answered a call that most people can't hear — and now the things in the dark hear it back.

Chimeras are not monsters. They appear mostly human, but with distinct bestial traits that refuse to be hidden: patches of fur along the jaw, goat-pupils in otherwise human eyes, hands that end in something a little too much like claws, antlers that grew in during adolescence and never stopped. The mutations vary. The social consequence is remarkably consistent.

* **BEAST BLOOD (Adaptation):** Choose one biological mutation:
    * **Predator:** Your Unarmed attacks deal **d6 Damage** (Claws/Fangs). Counts as a weapon.
    * **Night-Eyes:** You see perfectly in dim light and starlight. Darkness does not impose penalties on your checks.
    * **Thick Hide:** You ignore the effects of extreme Cold or Heat. Environmental Stress from temperature is halved.
* **BEACON (Magical Resonance):** Your blood sings to the ether. Spirits, Murk-Entities, and supernatural enemies always prioritize you as a target if you are within range. You cannot hide from magical detection — Relic Compasses point toward you as readily as they point toward active spells.

**On the Shelf:** Tolerated, mostly. Novemgrod is too desperate to be particularly picky about who keeps the fog at bay, and a Chimera with useful skills gets hired despite the unease they produce. Outside the city, the reception varies wildly: the Nine Mothers' faithful treat Chimeras as sacred kin; parts of the Choir consider them walking evidence of heretical pacts; the Carantha mercantile class cares about your output, not your appearance. On the Shelf, you are an edge case that most institutions have chosen to handle on a case-by-case basis, which means your experience depends almost entirely on who you are talking to.

**In the Murk:** The Beacon drawback is not cosmetic. In the deep Murk — where most missions go — supernatural presences are the rule, not the exception. A Chimera in the party will be targeted first by anything that hunts by magical resonance, which is nearly everything below the fog-line. This reshapes combat positioning: a Chimera Soldier *wants* to be in front and targeted. A Chimera Ruffian who relies on stealth must plan around the fact that the shadows know where they are even when the enemies don't.

The upside is real, too. Some Murk-entities will not attack a Chimera they recognize as kin. This does not mean they are safe — it means they might be spoken to first.

## A NOTE ON DIVERGENCE

The species listed here are not the only Divergences that exist on Tellus. They are the ones common enough to have established mechanical frameworks. A Director running a campaign in the deep Kemet ruins or the Suomi interior might introduce species that don't appear on this list — remnant populations adapted to conditions the Shelf has never seen. The framework above (one Advantage, one Drawback, one social context) is a template as much as a ruleset.

What matters is that every Divergence costs something. The Murk does not hand out advantages without a price attached.
